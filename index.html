Introduction

Aero Insights is a data-driven exploration into aviation safety. This project analyzes trends in aircraft accidents alongside airport traffic data to uncover actionable insights that can help improve aviation safety across the United States.

We combined two key datasets, one detailing aviation accidents and another tracking airport traffic volume â€” to identify patterns, potential risk factors, and correlations. By doing so, we aim to highlight critical safety considerations based on when, where, and how incidents occur.

Power BI Analysis (Questions 1-5)

Our Power BI dashboard provides an overview of accident trends and influencing factors:

  • Q1: How do accidents vary over time?
  • Q2: Which weather conditions lead to more severe accidents?
  • Q3: What are the top locations for accidents?
  • Q4: Which aircraft maker has the most accidents?
  • Q5: How does engine count influence accident severity?

Key Insights (Power BI)

  • Q1: Aviation accidents have significantly decreased over the past four decades, with a peak of 2128 accidents in 1980 dropping to just 55 by 2020, likely due to improved safety regulations, technology, and training implemented over the years.
  • Q2: While VMC conditions account for the majority of accidents (2695), IMC conditions have a higher proportion of fatal accidents, with 62.6% of IMC accidents being fatal (228 out of 364) compared to 52.6% in VMC (1418 out of 2695), highlighting IMC as a critical risk factor for severe outcomes.
  • Q3: Anchorage, AK, stands out as the top location for aviation accidents with 395 incidents, more than double the number in Chicago, IL (160), likely due to its challenging weather and terrain, highlighting the need for enhanced safety measures in such high-risk areas.
  • Q4: Cessna aircraft lead with 313 accidents, of which 80% (250) are fatal, indicating a high risk associated with this manufacturer, possibly due to their widespread use in general aviation.
  • Q5: Single-engine aircraft dominate accident statistics, accounting for 84.87% of incidents (14,874), suggesting that their design or operational use may pose higher risks compared to multi-engine aircraft.
